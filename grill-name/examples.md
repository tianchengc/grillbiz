# Startup Brand Naming Styles

When creating a startup name, you should generate options across several distinct styles. This document details the 5 core naming styles used by successful businesses, including real-world case studies and brand logic.

---

## Style 1: Abstract & Coined (Neologisms)
* **Concept:** Invented words with no pre-existing dictionary meaning.
* **Why it works:** They are highly brandable, completely unique, and relatively easy to trademark globally. They allow you to define the word's meaning from scratch.
* **Examples:**
  - **Google:** A deliberate misspelling of *Googol* (the number 1 followed by 100 zeros), representing the company's mission to organize immense amounts of information.
  - **Nvidia:** Derived from *Invidia*, the Latin word for "envy," combined with "NV" (Next Version) to represent looking forward.
  - **Kodak:** Coined by George Eastman simply because he liked the letter "K," which he felt was "strong and incisive."
  - **Grok:** A word coined by sci-fi author Robert A. Heinlein in *Stranger in a Strange Land*, meaning to understand something profoundly.

---

## Style 2: Suggestive & Evocative (Metaphorical)
* **Concept:** Real words that hint at the core essence, speed, strength, or benefit of the business without literally describing it.
* **Why it works:** Bridges meaning with creative expression. Evokes imagery and feeling, making the brand feel more human.
* **Examples:**
  - **Stripe:** Evocative of a clean line, connection, or swiping a card. It suggests simplicity and structural alignment.
  - **Slack:** Playfully stands for *"Searchable Log of All Conversation and Knowledge"*, but also functions metaphorically to suggest creating "slack" (breathing room or flexibility) in your team's day.
  - **Nike:** Named after the Greek goddess of victory, evoking speed, performance, and winning.
  - **Amazon:** Named after the largest river in the world, representing the goal of building a massive, flowing bookstore/marketplace.

---

## Style 3: Out-of-Context Nouns (Tactile Metaphors)
* **Concept:** Everyday household items, plants, or fruits that have zero relationship to the industry.
* **Why it works:** Counters cold, clinical, or dry industry jargon by introducing warmth, physical touch, and approachability.
* **Examples:**
  - **Apple:** Steve Jobs wanted something that sounded friendly, organic, and "not corporate," to stand in opposition to three-letter tech acronyms like IBM.
  - **BlackBerry:** The keyboard buttons resembled the surface of a blackberry, giving it a friendly, tactile quality.
  - **Caterpillar:** The physical crawling movement of the tractor tracks looked like a caterpillar, transforming a heavy machinery brand into an organic, memorable icon.

---

## Style 4: Compound & Portmanteaus (Blends)
* **Concept:** Welding two distinct words or word fragments together to form a new compound name.
* **Why it works:** Describes the function of the business in a highly compressed, catchy way.
* **Examples:**
  - **Cisco:** A compressed portmanteau of the city of **San Francisco**, where the founders lived.
  - **Microsoft:** A blend of **Microcomputer** and **Software**.
  - **SpaceX:** Short for **Space Exploration**.
  - **Instagram:** A blend of **Instant camera** and **Telegram**.
  - **Pinterest:** A blend of **Pin** and **Interest**.

---

## Style 5: Descriptive (Literal)
* **Concept:** Standard descriptive combinations that state exactly what the product or business does.
* **Why it works:** Extremely easy for customers to understand immediately. Requires very little explanation. However, they can be difficult to trademark because they are generic.
* **Examples:**
  - **PayPal:** Described a pal or friend that helps you pay.
  - **Salesforce:** Explicitly described a system to empower your sales force.
  - **General Motors:** Described a general-purpose motor manufacturing company.
